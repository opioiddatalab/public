Well, duh.

Nobody alive in 2020 will be shocked to hear that one's ability to social distance is related to financial and social privilege.

https://www.nytimes.com/2020/04/05/opinion/coronavirus-social-distancing.html

Our goal was to quantify specific structural factors that may have made social distancing easier for some.

 

The implications of this research are on intervention evaluation, confounding of relative infection incidence rates, and equity in making policies for reopening.

primary results

privilege in healthcare and social indicators

There were strong linear gradients across all eleven indicators.

 

Healthier and wealthier counties implemented social distancing with more intensity than less privileged counties.

Healthcare

Compared to the least social distancing areas, the most social distancing areas had:

  • 52% less uninsured
  • 47% more primary care providers
  • 6.2% higher influenza vaccination

Quantifying privilege

After adjusting for rurality and stay-at-home orders, counties with the highest intensity social distancing had:

  • 52% less uninsured
  • 47% more primary care providers
  • 29% more exercise space
  • 27% less food insecurity
  • 26% less child poverty
  • 17% higher incomes
  • 14% less overcrowding
  • 9.6% more racial segregation
  • 8.2% less youth
  • 7.4% more elderly
  • 6.2% more influenza vaccination

Validation using structural equation models for correlation and 95% CI accounting for repeated observations

Limitations

Change in movement during the early stages of the pandemic served as a reasonable proxy for one type of social distancing.

More localized social distancing efforts were not measured. 

Extremely rural areas with less than ten 8h traces/day were excluded.

Work-from-home jobs are less abundant in rural areas. But we compared within levels of rurality. Commuting times did not further improve model fit.
